\begin{abstract}
Monetary risk measures have gained popularity in expressing decision-makers' preferences for risk aversion. Value-at-Risk (VaR) and Conditional-Value-at-Risk are very common risk measures. This paper proposes novel algorithms for computing these risk measures for discrete distributions in linear time. Our algorithms improve on existing algorithms---which must sort the random variable---by at least a logarithmic factor. Our algorithms adapt the quickselect algorithm and combine it with a general polymatroid representation of $\varphi$-divergence risk measures. The numerical results show that our new algorithms offer an order of magnitude speed up for large domains and a library implementation of the algorithms is available at \textcolor{green}{REDACTED}.     
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

Monetary measures of risk generalize the expectation operator to be able to represent the risk aversion of decision makers in domains that range from robotics~\cite{} to finance~\cite{} to infrastructure maintenance~\cite{}. Value-at-Risk~(VaR) and Conditional-Value-at-Risk~(CVaR) are the most popular risk measures and have been making inroads in reinforcement learning, machine learning, and decision-making~\cite{Petrik2024}. Machine learning and reinforcement learning algorithms typically must compute the risk measures for discrete random variables. Fast algorithms for computing risk measures are necessary in several machine-learning settings~\cite{}. For example, when used as a part of an optimization algorithm, the risk measure may need to be evaluated in every iteration of the algorithm and can represent a significant computational bottleneck~\cite{}. 


%However, in many applications such as robotics, finance, and autonomous systems, solutions must account for performance with respect to worst case scenarios rather than simply maximizing the mean. Therefore, it has become a common practice to optimize policies with respect to a risk measure~\cite{Follmer2016}, which focuses on the tails of the reward distribution rather than the expectation. 

% what are we doing; our contributions
This paper proposes new fast algorithms for computing risk measures of discrete random variables. First, we propose a new linear-time VaR evaluation algorithm. Our VaR algorithm, which we call \emph{quickquantile}, builds on the well-known ideas of quickselect but generalizes it to quantiles. The worst-case linear-time upper bound of \emph{quickquantile} matches the linear-time lower bound~\cite{}. Second, building on \emph{quickquantile} we propose a general algorithm for evaluating some $\varphi$-divergence risk measures~\cite{}, which include CVaR and Total-Value-at-Risk (TVaR)~\cite{}. The algorithm, which we call \emph{quickdivergence}, works by representing certain $\varphi$-divergence risk measures as a linear minimization over polymatroids. The linear worst-case runtime of \emph{quickdivergence} also matches the known lower bounds on the runtime~\cite{}. 

There is limited prior work on fast algorithms for evaluating risk measures. The conventional VaR algorithm for a discrete random variable must sort its values before it can evaluate its risk. As a result, the sort's runtime, which requires $\Omega(n \log n)$ operations where $n$ is the number of the probability mass function~(pmf) entries, dominates the runtime complexity of VaR. To the best of our knowledge at the time of writing, no prior work has proposed or studied linear-time algorithms for any of the above risk measures.

Coherent risk measures, like CVaR and TVaR, can be evaluated by solving convex optimization problems~\cite{}. Although most of these optimization problems are tractable, their time complexity is too large to be practical. Most practical implementations rely on specific properties of these optimization problems to compute the solution more efficiently. For example, all existing CVaR algorithms must sort the values of the random variable to compute its value~\cite{}, as in the case for VaR. The $\Omega(n \log  n)$ time complexity of the sort dominates the time complexity and prevents linear time computation. In this paper, we circumvent the complexity of sorting by developing no-sort algorithms.

Numerous efficient algorithms have been designed for structured optimization problems that are similar to CVaR and TVaR evaluations. Some example of such structured optimization problems are the projection onto the simplex~\cite{adam2019projectionscanonicalsimplexadditional}, projection on k-capped simplex~\cite{kcapped_ang}, linear optimization on the intersection of $L_p$ or $\varphi$-divergence balls and the probability simplex as arise in robust optimizations~\cite{}, optimization for $\varphi$-divergence risk measures~\cite{}, and on polytopes defined by submodular functions which are known as polymatroids~\cite{}. Many of these algorithms require sorting the input variables, which has $O(n \log  n)$ complexity. There has been mention of the possibility of linear time algorithms however, we could find neither work describing nor studying them. One exception is \cite{Condat2016}, which proposes a linear-time algorithm for computing the projection onto the simplex, which does not leverage quickselect. 

Our algorithms' time complexity improves on the state of the art by a logarithmic factor. Although this improvement may seem unimportant,  it is an acceleration that is practically meaningful without any serious drawbacks. Our algorithms are simple and easy to implement with little added complexity compared with the state-of-the-art. Our numerical results show that they can lead to an order of magnitude speed up for realistic large problems, a significant speed up for moderately sized problems, and match the performance of existing algorithms for small problems.

\section{Preliminaries: Monetary Risk Measures}\label{sec:preliminaries}

In this work, we restrict our attention to random variables defined on finite probability spaces $(\Omega, 2^{\Omega}, \bm{p})$ where $\Omega := \left\{ 1, \dots , n \right\}$, $\bm{p} \in \Delta_n$, and $\Delta_n$ is the $n$-dimensional probability simplex. We use a tilde to indicate a random variable, such as $\tilde{x}\colon \Omega \to \Real$. The set of real-valued random variables is denoted by $\mathbb{X}$. Vectors are denoted with a lower-case bold font, such as $\bm{x} \in \Real^n$ and $\bm{x}_i, i = 1, \dots , n$ is the $i$-th element of the vector. We use $\tilde{x}$ and $\bm{x}$ interchangeably, such that $\bm{x}_i = \tilde{x}(i), i\in 1, \dots , n$. Sets are denoted with calligraphic letters. Finally, we use $\bar{\Real} := \Real \cup \left\{ -\infty, \infty \right\}$ to denote the extended real line. 

\emph{Monetary risk measures} $\rho\colon \mathbb{X} \to \bar{\Real}$ generalize the expectation operator to account for the uncertainty in the distribution of a real-valued random variable~\cite{Follmer2016}. In this paper, we assume that the risk measures are applied to random variables that represent rewards and that a higher risk value is preferable to a lower risk value. 

\emph{Value-at-Risk}~(VaR) is one of the most popular and interpretable risk measures~\cite{Follmer2016,Shapiro2014}. It is defined for $\alpha\in [0,1)$ for any $\tilde{x}\in \mathbb{X}$ as~\cite{Follmer2016}
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:var-definition}
  \varo_{\alpha}(\tilde{x})
  \;:=\;
    \max\; \left\{\tau \in \bar{\Real} \mid \mathbb{P}[\tilde{x} < \tau]  \le \alpha\right\}.
\end{equation}
The maximum in~\eqref{eq:var-definition} exists because $\tau \mapsto \mathbb{P}[\tilde{x} < \tau]$ is a lower semi-continuous function. It is easy to see that $\varo_0[\tilde{x}] = \ess \inf [\tilde{x}]$, and typically the risk measures is extended as $\varo_{1}[ \tilde{x} ] = \infty$. The \emph{quantile} $\quant_{\alpha}$ for $\tilde{x}$ is defined as the set
\[
  \quant_{\alpha}[\tilde{x}] := \left\{ z\in \Real \mid  \P{ \tilde{x} \le z } \ge \alpha , \P{ \tilde{x} \ge z } \ge 1-\alpha  \right\},
  \quad
  \quant^+_{\alpha}[\tilde{x}] = \max \quant_{\alpha},
  \quad
  \quant^-_{\alpha}[\tilde{x}] = \min \quant_{\alpha}.
\]

We also consider the \emph{Conditional-Value-at-Risk}~(CVaR), a common risk measure, which is defined for a random variable $\tilde{x} \in \mathbb{X}$ and risk level $\alpha \in (0,1]$ as~\cite{Follmer2016}
\begin{equation} \label{eq:primal_cvar}
    \cvaro_\alpha(\tilde{x})
  := \max_{z \in \Real} \left(z - \alpha^{-1} \bm{p}\tr[z \cdot \bm{1} -\bm{x}]_+\right) 
  = \min \left\{\bm{q}\tr \bm{x} \mid \bm{q} \leq \alpha^{-1} \bm{p},\, \bm{q}\in \Delta_n \right\},
    %&= \min \left\{  \Ex{\bm{\xi}}{ \tilde{x}  } \mid
  %\bm{\xi} \leq \frac{1}{\alpha} p, \bm{\xi} \in \Delta^n \right\} \\
  %&= \max \left\{ z - \frac{1}{\alpha} \Ex{\bm{p}}{z-\bm{x}}_+ \mid z \in \Real\right\} \\
\end{equation}
where $\bm{1}$ is a vector of ones. CVaR is usually continuously extended as $\cvaro_0[\tilde{x}] = \ess\inf[\tilde{x}]$.

\emph{$\varphi$-divergence risk measures} generalize CVaR to a broader class of related risk measures~\cite{Ahmadi-Javid2017} as shown in \cref{tab:divergences}. A general divergence risk measure $\ER_{\varphi, \beta}\colon \mathbb{X} \to  \bar{\Real}$  is defined for risk parameters $\varphi\colon \Real \to \bar{\Real}$ and $\beta\in \Real_+$ as
\begin{align} \label{eq:fdiv-dual}
  \ER_{\varphi,\beta}(\tilde{x}) := \inf \left\{\bm{q}\tr \bm{x} \mid H_{\varphi}(\bm{q} \| \bm{p}) \le \beta, \bm{q} \ll \bm{p} \right\},
  \quad
  H_\varphi(\bm{q} \| \bm{p}) := \sum_{i \in \Omega} p_i \cdot \varphi\left(\frac{q_i}{p_i}\right),
\end{align} 
where $H_{\varphi}$ is a $\varphi$-divergence~\cite{Bayraksan2015}, and $\bm{q} \ll \bm{p}$ indicates that $\bm{q} \in \Delta_n$ and $p_i =0 \implies q_i = 0, \forall  i = \in \Omega$ The function $\varphi$ is convex and satisfies that $\varphi(1)=0$. Note that all $\varphi$-divergence risk measures also have the following equivalent dual representation~\cite{Ahmadi-Javid2011,Ahmadi-Javid2017}: 
\begin{equation} \label{eq:fdiv-primal}
\ER_{\varphi,\beta}(\tilde{x}) = \sup_{t,v \in \Real\,:\, t > 0}t \cdot \left(v - \E { \varphi\opt \left(- \frac{\tilde{x}}{t} + v + \beta \right)}\right) ,
\end{equation} 
where $\varphi\opt$ is the convex conjugate of $\varphi$. 

Finally, it is important to note that $\varphi$-divergence risk measures are coherent, which means they are convex and positively homogeneous. Any coherent risk measure $\rho\colon \mathbb{X}\to \bar{\Real}$ satisfies for some closed convex non-empty set $\mathcal{Q} \subseteq \Delta^n$ that~\citep[Prop. 2.84]{Follmer2016} 
\begin{equation*}
% \label{eq:correspondence_coherent_risk}
  \rho(\tilde{x}) =\inf_{\bm{q}\in \mathcal{Q}} \bm{q}\tr \bm{x}.
\end{equation*}

In the remainder of the paper, we first propose a linear-time algorithm for computing VaR and then we build on it to propose a linear time algorithm for certain $\varphi$-divergence risk measures.

 \item[Case 4]: Tail is selected such that $\alpha \geq t_k$. In this case assume $f_k$ is set to $ind + 1$ such that $f_k = b_k$.

\end{definition}

\begin{lemma}
  For $\mathcal{O} \subseteq \Omega$, define $f\colon 2^{\Omega} \to \Real_+$:
  \[
    f(\mathcal{O}) = \min\left\{\frac{\mathbb{P}[\mathcal{O}]}{\alpha}, 1\right\}
    .
  \]
  Then
  \[
  \cvar{\alpha }{\tilde{x} } = \max \left\{\bm{x}\tr \bm{q} \mid  \bm{q} \in \mathcal{Q}_f\right\}.
  \]
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
  Due to the set of constraints, we know that $f(\mathcal{O})$ must be $\leq \frac{\mathbb{P}[\mathcal{O}]}{\alpha}$ and $f(\mathcal{O})$ must be $\leq 1$. Furthermore, we can construct $q$ such that $q(\mathcal{O}) =\min\left\{\frac{\mathbb{P}[\mathcal{O}]}{\alpha}, 1\right\} $ for any $\mathcal{O}$.
\item[Case 1]: $P(\mathcal{O}) \geq \alpha$. In this scenario, $Q(\mathcal{O})$ = 1 since $\frac{1}{\alpha} P(\mathcal{O}) > 1 $. Thus, every element in $\mathcal{O}$ should be scaled by $\frac{1}{P(\mathcal{O})}$ and all other elements should be set to 0 . Since  $P(\mathcal{O}) \geq \alpha$, we can imply the reciprocal $\frac{1}{P(\mathcal{O})} \leq \frac{1}{1-\alpha}$ is also true.  
\item[Case 2]: $P(\mathcal{O}) < (\alpha)$. In this scenario, $Q(\mathcal{O}) \leq \frac{1}{1 - \alpha} \cdot  P(\mathcal{O}) < 1$. Then any given $\mathcal{O}$, we can construct a $Q(\mathcal{O})$ such that $Q(\mathcal{O}) = \frac{1}{\alpha} \cdot  P(\mathcal{O})$. 
This can be done by setting every element in $\mathcal{O}$ to $\frac{1}{\alpha} \cdot P(\omega)$ for all $\omega$ in $\Omega$ and $P(\omega) \cdot  d$ otherwise such that $\frac{1}{1 - \alpha} * P(\Omega) + d*P(B) = 1$, where $B$ contains all elements not in $\Omega$. It is provable that $d \leq \frac{1}{\alpha}$, which is required for the constructed $Q$ to be feasible. For all feasible $\alpha \geq 0$, $\frac{1}{\alpha} \ge 1$. Since $P(\Omega) + P(B) = 1$,  $d \le 1$ in order for $\frac{1}{\alpha} \cdot  P(\Omega) + d\cdot P(B) = 1$ to hold. Since $1 \leq \frac{1}{\alpha}$, this implies $d\le \leq \frac{1}{\alpha}$.
\end{proof}
We have now shown that $Q(\Omega) \leq f(\mathcal{O}) = \min\left\{\frac{\mathbb{P}[\mathcal{O}]}{\alpha}, 1\right\}$ and it is possible to construct $Q(\Omega)$ such that $Q(\Omega) = \min\left\{\frac{\mathbb{P}[\mathcal{O}]}{\alpha}, 1\right\}$. Thus by Remark 4.90 in Follmer and Schied, $f(\mathcal{O}) = \min\left\{\frac{\mathbb{P}[\mathcal{O}]}{\alpha}, 1\right\}$

\begin{algorithm}
\caption{VaR: Standard VaR algorithm}
\label{alg:slow-quantile}
\Input{vector $\bm{x}$, probability $\bm{p}$, and $\alpha \in [0,1)$}
\Output{$\alpha$-quantile of $\tilde{X}$}
Sort $\bm{x}$ indexes to get $\bm{k}\in \Nats^n$ such that $i > j \implies x_{k_i} > x_{k_j}, \; \forall i, j \in 1, \dots , n$\;
$q_{n+1} \gets 0$ \;
\For{$i=n, \dots , 1$} {
    $q_i \gets q_{i+1} + p_{k_i}$
    \tcp*{$q_i = \P{\tilde{x} \ge x_{k_i}}$ if $x_{k_i}$ unique}
    \lIf{$q_i \ge 1-\alpha$} {\KwRet{$x_{k_i}$}}
}
\end{algorithm}

\begin{proposition}
For a random variable $\tilde{x} \in \mathbb{X}$ and $\alpha\in [0,1)$, \cref{alg:slow-quantile} returns $\var{\alpha}{\tilde{x}}$.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
First, we show by induction on $i$ from $n$ to $1$ that
\begin{equation} \label{eq:proof-q-j}
  q_j = \P{\tilde{x} \ge x_{k_i}},
  \quad\text{where}\quad
  j = \min \left\{ j = 1, \dots , i \mid x_{k_j} = x_{k_i} \right\}.
\end{equation}
The base case is immediate. The inductive case for $i-1$ from $i$ follows by algebraic manipulation from the fact that $x_{k_i}$ is sorted increasingly:
\begin{align*}
\P{\tilde{x} \ge x_{k_{i-1}}} 
 &= \P{\tilde{x} = x_{k_{i-1}}} + \P{\tilde{x} > x_{k_{i-1}}}
 = \P{\tilde{x} = x_{k_{i-1}}} + \P{\tilde{x} \ge x_{k_{i}}} \\
 &= \P{\tilde{x} = x_{k_{i-1}}} + q_i = \sum_{l=j}^{i-1} q_{k_j} + q_{k_i} = q_{k_{i-1}}.
\end{align*}
Now, suppose that \cref{alg:slow-quantile} terminates with value $q\opt$. Then, if the algorithm terminates in iteration $i\opt$ then from \eqref{eq:proof-q-j}, we have that $\P{\tilde{x} \ge q\opt} = q_{j\opt} \ge 1-\alpha$ where $j\opt$ is defined as in \eqref{eq:proof-q-j}. Therefore, $q\opt \le \var{\alpha}{\tilde{x}}$. To prove the converse inequality, suppose that $q\opt > \var{\alpha}{\tilde{x}}$, and therefore $\P{\tilde{x} \ge q\opt} < 1-\alpha$. Then, this a contradition since \eqref{eq:proof-q-j} combined with the termination criterion implies that $\P{\tilde{x} \ge q\opt} < 1-\alpha$.
\end{proof}

\section{Comonotonicity of Risk}

\begin{definition}
Two random variables $\tilde{x}$ and $\tilde{y}$ are said to be comonotonic if
  \begin{align*}
    (\tilde{x}(\omega) - \tilde{x}(\omega'))(\tilde{y}(\omega) - \tilde{y}(\omega')) \geq 0 \quad \forall \; \omega, \omega' \in \Omega.
  \end{align*}
\end{definition}
\begin{definition}
  A risk measure is said to be comonotonic if given comonotonic random variables $\tilde{x}$ and $\tilde{y}$
  \begin{align*}
    \rho(\tilde{x} + \tilde{y}) = \rho(\tilde{x}) + \rho(\tilde{y}).
  \end{align*}
\end{definition}

\subsection{Computer Specifications}

The results obtained in this work were all generated on a computer with the following Specifications.
\begin{itemize}
  \item AMD Ryzen Thread ripper 3970X 32-Core (64) @ 4.55 GHz
  \item 256 GB RAM
  \item Linux 6.14.4-arch1-2 x86\_64
  \item Julia 1.11.5
\end{itemize}

The activity of other users on the system is minimized to account for scheduling conflicts.
The presence of multithreading in our language of choice (Julia) is not used in this work, as while
the runtime of the experiments would be shorter, we observed individual threads ``thrashing'', where 
they would suddenly take twice or three times as long on one particular iteration. 
We conjecture that this is due to the garbage collector. For this reason, we manually disable the 
Julia garbage collector, even when running synchronously.
