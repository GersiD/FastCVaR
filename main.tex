\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

% 1-2 paragraphs for general motivation ... what are risk measures and why they matter
In standard paradigms of reinforcement learning and decision making, it is common to maximize the expectation of the cumulative discounted future reward which depends on future states, policy actions, and translation dynamics~\cite{Sutton1998}. However, in many applications such as robotics, finance, and autonomous systems, solutions must account for performance with respect to worst case scenarios rather than simply maximizing the mean. Therefore, it has become a common practice to optimize policies with respect to a risk measure~\cite{Follmer2016}, which focuses on the tails of the reward distribution rather than the expectation. 

% what are we doing; our contributions 
In this work, we make  risk measures fast. Not just fast, very fast. We can solve several common risk measures in linear time, including VaR and CVaR. Our approach is general and encapsulates new cool amazing useful risk $\varphi$-divergence measures that can be solved in linear time. We call one of these TVaR and this matters because \todo{motivate} 

% how we are doing this? connect phi-divergence risk measures and polymatroids
For certain classes of polymatroids, we solve the Maximum-Weight-Independent-Sum (MWIS) problem in $O(N)$ time. In turn we demonstrate multiple examples of coherent and comonotonic risk measures which benifit from this observation. Finally for phi-divergence risk measures, which are a subset of coherent risk measures such that the divergence constraint is a phi-divergence, we establish the necessary conditions on the divergence in order for the resulting optimization problem to compute the risk measure be a MWIS.

% justify why this is important
In applications like finance, healthcare, and robotics, certain properties of risk measures have been established as desirable. These include positive homogeneity, subadditivity, translation invariance, and monotonicity~\citep{Follmer2016}. Risk measures that satisfy these properties are known as coherent risk measures. Coherent risk measures, when optimized, allow for policies which are risk-sensitive. In the applications of finance, healthcare, and robotics risk-sensitive policies often best match human preferences in their respective domains\todo{cite}. These human preferences, when summed, equate to ensuring good performance even in situations of uncertainty.

% justify why this is novel
Many applications of risk measures in the aformentioned fields require that they are efficiently computed. Compute time and resources are a major concern in real-world applications. For example, in finance, the computation of risk measures is often a bottleneck in portfolio optimization and risk management where trades are often made on intervals of milliseconds. Likewise in robotics, the computation of risk measures is often a bottleneck in real-time decision making. In healthcare, the computation of risk measures is often a bottleneck in clinical decision support systems which may care for a large quanity of patients.

For coherent risk measures, it has been shown that computing these risk measures is equivalent to solving a robust optimization problem where a probability distribution must be selected to minimize the expectation of a random variable subject to a divergence constraint from a normal distribution. For certain risk measures that satistfy an additional property known as comonotinicity, it is known that this optimization problem can be casted as the maximum weighted independent set problem (MWIS) on a polymathic.

Quantile of a random variable $\tilde{x} \in \mathbb{X}$ at level $\alpha \in [0,1]$ is any value $\tau\in \Real$ such that $\mathbb{P}({\tilde{x} \le \tau}) \ge \alpha$ and $\mathbb{P}({\tilde{x} \ge \tau}) \ge 1-\alpha$. The quantile may not be unique however always lies in the interval $[\quant^-_{\alpha}(\tilde{x}), \quant^+_{\alpha}(\tilde{x})]$ where
\begin{align} \label{eq:quantile-definition}
%\label{eq:upper-quantile-definition}
  \quant_{\alpha}^+(\tilde{x})
  &\;:=\;
    \max\; \left\{\tau \in \bar{\Real} \mid \mathbb{P}[\tilde{x} < \tau]  \le \alpha\right\}, 
      %\label{eq:low-quantile-definition}
  &\quant_{\alpha}^-(\tilde{x})
  &\;:=\;
    \min\; \left\{\tau \in \bar{\Real} \mid \mathbb{P}[\tilde{x} \le \tau]   \ge \alpha\right\}.
\end{align}
The maximum in~\eqref{eq:quantile-definition} exists because $\mathbb{P}[\tilde{x} < \tau]$ is lower semi-continuous and the minimum in~\eqref{eq:quantile-definition} exists because $\mathbb{P}[\tilde{x} \le \tau]$ is upper semi-continuous with respect to $\tau$. Note that $\quant^+_1(\tilde{x}) = \infty$, $\quant^-_0(\tilde{x}) = -\infty$, otherwise $\quant^+_\alpha, \quant^-_\alpha$ are bounded for all $\alpha \in (0,1)$ and $\tilde{x} \in \mathcal{L}^\infty$.

Monetary risk measures generalize the expectation operator to allow for risk sensitive distinctions in otherwise equivalent distributions~\cite{Follmer2016}.~\emph{Value-at-Risk}~(VaR) is one of the oldest, simplest, and most common risk measures~\cite{Follmer2016,Shapiro2014}. $\varo : \mathbb{X} \to \bar{\Real}$ at a risk level $\alpha \in [0,1]$ of a random variable $\tilde{x}\in \mathbb{X}$ is defined as:
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:var-definition}
\varo_{\alpha}(\tilde{x}) \;:=\;  \quant^+_{ \alpha }(\tilde{x}).  
\end{equation}

\paragraph{$\varphi$-divergence Risk Measures}

$\varphi$-divergences can be defined as 
\[
  I_\varphi(p \| q) = \sum_{\omega \in \Omega } q_{\omega } \cdot \varphi\left(\frac{p_\omega}{q_\omega}\right)
\] 
for two discrete, absolutely continuous probability distributions $p$ and $q$, and convex $\phi$ such that $\phi(1)=0$. 



Recall that any coherent risk measure can be defined as 
\begin{align}\label{eq:correspondence_coherent_risk}
  \rho(X)=\sup_{Q\in \mathcal{Q}}\mathbb{E}_Q [-X],
\end{align}
where $\mathcal{Q}$ is a subset of all probability measures on $\Omega$~\cite[Prop. 2.84]{Follmer2016}.
\[
 \mathcal{Q} := \left\{ q \in \Real^{\Omega} \mid I_{\varphi}(p \| q) \le \beta  \right\} 
\]
Phi-divergence risk measures can be defined as a coherent risk measure for which $\mathcal{Q}$ contains all $Q$ such that $I_\phi(P, Q) \le \beta$, where $\beta$ is a constant typically dependent on the risk level. $\varphi$-entropic (also known as $g$-entropic risk measures) can be seen as the primal representation of these risk measures. All $\varphi$-entropic risk measures take on the following form for $\beta \in \Real$ and any proper, lower semi-continuous, and convex function $\varphi\colon \Real \to \bar{\Real}$~\cite{Ahmadi-Javid2011,Ahmadi-Javid2017}: 
\[
\ER_{g,\beta}(\tilde{x}) = \sup_{t > 0, v \in \Real} t \left(v + \E { - \varphi\opt \left(-\frac{\tilde{x}}{t} + v + \beta \right)}\right),
\] 
where $\varphi^{\star}$ the convex conjugate of the $\varphi$-divergence $\varphi$. EVaR, for example, is a well known example of phi-divergence risk measure where the phi-Divergence is the KL-divergence~\cite{Ahmadi-Javid2017}.

The primal formulation is
\[
\ER_{g,\beta}(\tilde{x}) = \inf \left\{q\tr x \mid I_{\varphi}(p \| q) \le \beta,   q\in \Real^N \right\}   
\]

\paragraph{Polymatroids}

Let $\mathcal{E}$ be a finite set, and $f : \{\mathcal{X} \mid \mathcal{X} \subseteq \mathcal{E} \} \to \Real_+$ be a non-decreasing submodular function. That is, for each $\mathcal{A} \subseteq \mathcal{B} \subseteq \mathcal{E}$ we have $f(\mathcal{A}) \leq f(\mathcal{B})$, and for each $\mathcal{A}, \mathcal{B} \subseteq \mathcal{E}$ we have 
\[
f(\mathcal{A}) + f(\mathcal{B}) \geq f(\mathcal{A} \cup \mathcal{B}) + f(\mathcal{A} \cap \mathcal{B}).
\]
We define the polymatroid associated to $f$ to be the following polytope:
\[
  Q_f = \left\{ \bm{q} \in \Real_+^E  \mid  \sum_{e \in \mathcal{U}} \bm{q}(e) \leq f(\mathcal{U}), \;\; \forall \; \mathcal{U} \subseteq \mathcal{E} \right\}.
\]
This polytope is of special interest for computing risk measures, as we will see later on that all coherent and comonotonic risk measures can be computed by solving
\begin{align}\label{eq:polytope}
  \max_{q}\left\{\bm{x}\tr \bm{q} \mid  \bm{q} \in Q_f\right\}
\end{align}

\begin{theorem}[Corollary 4.95 in~\cite{Follmer2016}]
  For any coherent and comonotonic risk measure $\rho\colon \mathbb{X}\to \Real$, let $c:= \rho(\one_{\mathcal{A}})$ for each $\mathcal{A} \subseteq \Omega$. Then
  \[
   \rho(\tilde{x}) = \sup_{q\in \mm{??}} q\tr  x.
  \]
\end{theorem}

Our QVaR algorithm is described in \cref{alg:quick_quantile}. The algorithm works as follows. \mm{refer to the actual variables and line numbers in the algorithm.} The algorithm picks a pivot and applies the partition in order to easily compute the probability of all outcomes smaller than or equal to that of the pivot. Then it determines whether the quantile can exist on the left side of the pivot or on the right side and shrinks the search space, parameterized by the variables $front$ and $back$, accordingly.
\begin{algorithm}
  \algrenewcommand\algorithmicrequire{\textbf{Input: vector $x$, pmf $p$ and $\alpha \in [0,1]$}}
  \algrenewcommand\algorithmicensure{\textbf{Output: $\alpha$-quantile of $\tilde{X}$}}
  \caption{QVaR: Linear-time algorithm for VaR. $\mathds{1}(bool)$ denotes the indicator function.}\label{alg:quick_quantile}

  \begin{algorithmic}[1]
    \Require{} \Ensure{}
    \State{$f_0 \gets 1$, $b_0 \gets n$, $x^0 = x$, $p^0 = p$, $k=1$, $\alpha_0 = \alpha$ } \Comment{Init front $f_0$ and back $b_0$}
    \While{$b_k - f_k \geq 1$}
      \State{$i_k, x^k, p^k \gets \operatorname{hoare\_partition}(x^{k-1}, p^{k-1}, f_{k-1}, b_{k-1})$} %\Comment{Hoares partition scheme}}
    \State{$t_k \gets \sum_{j=f_{k-1}}^{i_k} p^k_j$}
      \If{$\alpha_k < t_k$}
      \State{$b_k \gets i_k$, $\alpha_k \gets \alpha_{k-1}$}
      \Else{}
      \State{$f_k \gets i_k + 1$, $\alpha_k \gets \alpha_{k-1} - t_k$}
    \EndIf{}
    \State $k \gets k + 1$
    \EndWhile{}
    \State{\Return{$x^k_{b_k}$}}
  \end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}



\begin{lemma}
The Hoare's partition scheme $i_k, x^k, p^k \gets \operatorname{partition}(x^{k-1}, p^{k-1}, f_{k-1}, b_{k-1})$ does the following:
  \begin{enumerate}
  \item $j \le i_k \Rightarrow x^k_j \le x^k_{i_k}$
  \item $j > i_k \Rightarrow x^k_j > x^k_{i_k}$
  \item $\exists l \ge i_k$ such that $ l \le j \le i_k \Rightarrow x^k_{i_k} = x^k_j $ and $ j < l \Rightarrow x^k_{i_k} > x^k_j $
  \item the algorithm runs in time linear (if mean of means pivot scheme) in $b_k - f_k$
  \end{enumerate}
  Note that only the elements $x^k_j$ for $j = f_k, \dots, b_k$ makes it possible to achieve the desired time complexity.
\end{lemma}
The above conditions primarily state that each call to the partition scheme performs partial sorting such that all elements less than or equal to the pivot will be at a lower index, and elements greater than the pivot will be at a higher index.

\subsection{CVaR}

As observed in~\cite{rockefeller1996variational}, the Conditional Value-at-Risk (CVaR) is a convex risk measure that generalizes Value-at-Risk (VaR) and can be defined as
\begin{equation} \label{eq:primal_cvar}
  \begin{aligned}
    \cvaro_\alpha(\tilde{x})
  &= \min \left\{  \Ex{\bm{\xi}}{ \tilde{x}  } \mid
  \bm{\xi} \leq \frac{1}{1-\alpha} p, \bm{\xi} \in \Delta^n \right\} \\
  &= \min \left\{\bm{\xi}\tr \bm{x} \mid \bm{\xi} \leq \frac{1}{1-\alpha} \bm{p},
     \xi\tr \bm{1} = 1, \bm{\xi} \geq 0\right\}\\
  &= \max \left\{ z - \frac{1}{1-\alpha} \Ex{\bm{p}}{z-\bm{x}}_+ \mid z \in \Real\right\} \\
  &= \max \left\{ z - \frac{1}{1-\alpha} \bm{p}\tr(z-\bm{x})_+ \mid z \in \Real\right\}.
  \end{aligned}
\end{equation}
Here, $\bm{1}$ is a vector of ones of an appropriate dimension. 

\begin{lem}
For $\omega \in \Omega$ we can construct a feasible\todo{what does feasible mean?} $Q \in \mathcal{Q}$ such that $Q(\omega) = \min\left\{\frac{P(\omega)}{1-\alpha}, 1\right\}$.
\end{lem}
\begin{proof}
Two possible scenarios exist.
\item[Case 1]: $P(A) \geq 1 - \alpha$. In this scenario, $Q(A)$ must equal 1 since $\frac{1}{1 - \alpha} P(A) > 1 $. Thus, every element in $A$ should be scaled by $\frac{1}{P(A)}$ if in $A$ and set to 0 otherwise. Since  $P(A) \geq 1 - \alpha$, we can imply the reciprocal $\frac{1}{P(A)} \leq \frac{1}{1-\alpha}$ is also true.  
\item[Case 2]: $P(A) < (1 - \alpha)$. In this scenario, $Q(A) \leq \frac{1}{1 - \alpha} \cdot  P(A) < 1$. For any given $A$, we can construct a Q(A) such that $Q(A) = \frac{1}{1 - \alpha} \cdot  P(A)$. This can be done by setting $Q(a)$ to $\frac{1}{1 - \alpha} \cdot P(a)$ for all $a$ in $A$ and $P(a) \cdot  d$ otherwise such that $\frac{1}{1 - \alpha} * P(A) + d*P(B) = 1$, where $B$ contains all elements not in $A$. It is provable that $d \leq \frac{1}{1 - \alpha}$, which is required for the constructed \(Q\) to be feasible. For all feasible $\alpha \geq 0$, $\frac{1}{1 - \alpha} \ge 1$. Since $P(A) + P(B) = 1$,  $d \le 1$ in order for $\frac{1}{1 - \alpha} \cdot  P(A) + d\cdot P(B) = 1$ to hold. Since $1 \leq \frac{1}{1-\alpha}$, this implies $d\le \leq \frac{1}{1 - \alpha}$.
\end{proof}

\begin{assumption}
Assume for $P_f$ that $f(U)$ is such that for all $S_i, S_{ i+1 } \cup E$ where $S_i \cup (i + 1) = S_{ i+1 } $ and $f(S_{ i+1 }) < f(E)$, $f(S_i) + f({i + 1}) = f(S_{ i+1 })$.
\end{assumption}
We note this assumption essentially state that the submodular function must be a piecewise linear function.

Algorithm 1 returns a feasible and optimal solution to the maximum weight independent set for polymatroids.
\end{lem}
\begin{proof}
The optimal solution will always be $f(S_i) - f(S_{i-1})$ assuming the weight $c_i \geq 0$. Our solution is $f(i)$ for $f(S_i) < f(N)$, and by assumption 1 this equals $f(S_i) - f(S_{i - 1})$. If $f(S_i) = f(N)$ and $f(S_{i - 1}) = f(N)$, then $x_i = 0$ by the algorithm.
\end{proof}

\begin{definition}
Two random variables $\tilde{x}$ and $\tilde{y}$ are said to be comonotonic if
  \begin{align*}
    (\tilde{x}(\omega) - \tilde{x}(\omega'))(\tilde{y}(\omega) - \tilde{y}(\omega')) \geq 0 \quad \forall \; \omega, \omega' \in \Omega.
  \end{align*}
\end{definition}
\begin{definition}
  A risk measure is said to be comonotonic if given comonotonic random variables $\tilde{x}$ and $\tilde{y}$
  \begin{align*}
    \rho(\tilde{x} + \tilde{y}) = \rho(\tilde{x}) + \rho(\tilde{y}).
  \end{align*}
\end{definition}
