\begin{abstract}
Monetary risk measures have gained popularity in expressing decision-makers' preferences for risk aversion. Value-at-Risk (VaR) and Conditional-Value-at-Risk (CVaR) are very common risk measures. This paper proposes novel algorithms for computing these risk measures for discrete distributions in linear time. Our algorithms improve upon existing algorithms, which must sort the random variable by at least a logarithmic factor. We adapt the QuickSelect algorithm and combine it with a general polymatroid representation of $\varphi$-divergence risk measures. Numerical results show that our new algorithms offer an order of magnitude speed up for large domains, and a library implementation of the algorithms is available at \textcolor{green}{REDACTED}.     
\end{abstract}

Monetary measures of risk generalize the expectation operator to be able to represent the risk aversion of decision makers in domains that range from robotics~\cite{akella2024risk,majumdar2020should,benrabah2024review} to finance~\cite{Follmer2016} to infrastructure maintenance~\cite{}. Value-at-Risk~(VaR) and Conditional-Value-at-Risk~(CVaR) are the most popular risk measures and have made inroads in reinforcement learning, machine learning, and decision making~\cite{Petrik2024}. Machine learning and reinforcement learning algorithms typically must compute the risk measures for discrete random variables. Fast algorithms for computing risk measures are necessary in several machine-learning settings~\cite{Ho2021}. For example, when used as part of an optimization algorithm, the risk measure may need to be evaluated in every iteration of the algorithm and can represent a significant computational bottleneck~\cite{Ho2021}.

This paper proposes fast algorithms for computing risk measures of discrete random variables. First, we show VaR can be seen as a special case of the WeightedMedian problem, which has well known linear time algorithms.
%The worst-case linear-time upper bound of \emph{QuickQuantile} matches the linear-time lower bound~\cite{}. 
Second, we propose a general algorithm for evaluating some $\varphi$-divergence risk measures~\cite{Ahmadi-Javid2012}, which include CVaR and the $\varphi$-divergence which uses the unweighted L1 norm as the divergence - which we will henceforth refer to as (TVaR)~\cite{Kruse2021}. The algorithm, which we call \emph{QuickDivergence}, works by representing certain $\varphi$-divergence risk measures as a linear minimization over polymatroids. The linear worst-case runtime of \emph{QuickDivergence} also matches the known lower bounds on the runtime~\cite{}. 

There is limited prior work on fast algorithms for evaluating risk measures. The conventional VaR algorithm for a discrete random variable must sort its values before it can evaluate its risk. As a result, the sort's runtime, which requires   $\Omega(n \log n)$ operations where $n$ is the number of the probability mass function~(pmf) entries, dominates the runtime complexity of VaR. To the best of our knowledge at the time of writing, no prior work has proposed or studied linear-time algorithms for any of the above risk measures.

Coherent risk measures, like CVaR and TVaR, can be evaluated by solving convex optimization problems~\cite{Follmer2016, rockefeller1996variational}. Although most of these optimization problems are tractable, their time complexity is too large to be practical for applications where they are most needed. Current practical implementations rely on specific properties of these optimization problems to compute the solution efficiently. For example, all existing CVaR algorithms must sort the values of the random variable to compute its value~\cite{}, as in the case for VaR. The $\Omega(n \log  n)$ time complexity of the sort dominates the time complexity and prevents linear time computation. In this paper, we circumvent the complexity of sorting by developing no-sort algorithms for these risk measures and others. 

Numerous efficient algorithms have been designed for structured optimization problems that are similar to CVaR and TVaR evaluations. Some example of such structured optimization problems are the projection onto the simplex~\cite{adam2019projectionscanonicalsimplexadditional, Wang2013Simplex}, projection on k-capped simplex~\cite{kcapped_ang}, linear optimization on the intersection of $L_p$ or $\varphi$-divergence balls and the probability simplex as arise in robust optimizations~\cite{}, optimization for $\varphi$-divergence risk measures~\cite{}, and on polytopes defined by submodular functions which are known as polymatroids~\cite{}. Many of these algorithms require sorting the input variables, which has $O(n \log  n)$ complexity. There has been mention of the possibility of linear time algorithms however, we could find neither work describing nor studying them. One exception is \cite{Condat2016}, which proposes a linear-time algorithm for computing the projection onto the simplex, which does not leverage QuickSelect. 

Our algorithms' time complexity improves on the state of the art by a logarithmic factor. Although this improvement may seem unimportant,  it is an acceleration that is practically meaningful without any serious drawbacks. Our algorithms are simple and easy to implement with little added complexity compared with the state-of-the-art. Our numerical results show that they can lead to an order of magnitude speed up for realistic large problems, a significant speed-up for moderately sized problems, and match the performance of existing algorithms for small problems.

\section{Preliminaries}\label{sec:preliminaries}

In this work, we restrict our attention to random variables defined on finite probability spaces $(\Omega, 2^{\Omega}, \bm{p})$ where $\Omega := \left\{ 1, \dots , n \right\}$, $\bm{p} \in \Delta_n$, and $\Delta_n$ is the $n$-dimensional probability simplex. We use a tilde to indicate a random variable, such as $\tilde{x}\colon \Omega \to \Real$. The set of real-valued random variables is denoted by $\mathbb{X}$. Vectors are denoted with a lower-case bold font, such as $\bm{x} \in \Real^n$ and indexed as $\bm{x}_i \text{ for } i = 1, \dots , n$. We use $\tilde{x}$ and $\bm{x}$ interchangeably, such that $\bm{x}_\omega = \tilde{x}(\omega) \text{ for }  \omega \in \Omega$. Sets are denoted with calligraphic letters and $\bar{\Real} := \Real \cup \left\{ -\infty, \infty \right\}$ denotes the extended real line. 

\emph{Monetary risk measures} $\rho\colon \mathbb{X} \to \bar{\Real}$ generalize the expectation operator to account for different notions of uncertainty in the distribution of a random variable~\cite{Follmer2016}. We apply risk measures to rewards and suppose that higher risk values are preferable. 

First, we consider the \emph{Value-at-Risk}~(VaR)---one of the most popular risk measures---that is defined for $\alpha\in [0,1)$ for any $\tilde{x}\in \mathbb{X}$ as~\cite{Follmer2016,Shapiro2014}
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:var-definition}
  \var{\alpha}{ \tilde{x} }
  \;:=\;
    \max\; \left\{\tau \in \bar{\Real} \mid \P{\tilde{x} < \tau}  \le \alpha\right\} =
    \max\; \left\{\tau \in \bar{\Real} \mid \P{\tilde{x} \ge  \tau}  \ge 1 - \alpha\right\}.
\end{equation}
The first maximum in~\eqref{eq:var-definition} exists because $\tau \mapsto \P{\tilde{x} < \tau}$ is a lower semi-continuous function. It is easy to see that $\varo_0[\tilde{x}] = \ess \inf [\tilde{x}]$, and typically the risk measure is extended as $\varo_{1}[ \tilde{x} ] = \infty$. Our analysis will also require the notion of the \emph{quantile} $\quant_{\alpha}$ for $\tilde{x}$ which is defined as the set
\[
  \quant_{\alpha}[\tilde{x}] := \left\{ z\in \Real \mid  \P{ \tilde{x} \le z } \ge \alpha , \P{ \tilde{x} \ge z } \ge 1-\alpha  \right\}.
\]
The lower and upper bounds of the quantile set are denoted by $\quant^+_{\alpha}[\tilde{x}] := \max \quant_{\alpha}[\tilde{x}] = \var{\alpha }{\tilde{x}}$ and $\quant^-_{\alpha}[\tilde{x}] := \min \quant_{\alpha}[\tilde{x}]$.

Second, we consider \emph{$\varphi$-divergence risk measures}~\cite{Ahmadi-Javid2012}. A divergence risk measure $\ER_{\varphi, \beta }\colon \mathbb{X} \to  \bar{\Real}$  is defined for a closed, convex function $\varphi\colon \bar{\Real} \to \bar{\Real}$ that satisfies $\varphi(1) = 0$ and $\beta\in \Real_{\ge 0}$ as
\begin{align} \label{eq:fdiv-primal}
  \ER_{\varphi, \beta}[ \tilde{x} ]
  &:= \inf \left\{\bm{q}\tr \bm{x} \mid \bm{q}\in \Delta_n, H_{\varphi}(\bm{q} \| \bm{p}) \le \beta
    %, \bm{q} \ll \bm{p}
    \right\} \\
  \label{eq:fdiv-dual}
  &= \sup \left\{  t \cdot v - t\cdot \E { \varphi\opt \left(- \frac{\tilde{x}}{t} + v + \beta \right)} \mid  t,v \in \Real, \, t > 0 \right\}.
\end{align}
% The notation $\bm{q} \ll \bm{p}$ indicates that $\bm{q}$ is absolutely continuous with respect to $\bm{p}$: $p_{\omega} =0 \implies q_{\omega} = 0, \forall  \omega \in \Omega$.

\[
  H_\varphi(\bm{q} \| \bm{p}) := \sum_{\omega \in \Omega} p_{\omega} \cdot \varphi\left(\frac{q_{\omega}}{p_{\omega}}\right),
\]
and we adopt the common convention that $1 / 0 = \infty$ and $0 \cdot \infty = 0$~\cite{rockefeller1996variational}. Note that the absolute continuity restriction is not needed for finite probability spaces.

\section{Computing VaR in Linear Time} \label{sec:var-linear}

In this section, we show the equivalence between VaR and the weighted-median problem, which is well known to be solvable in linear time \cite{korte2011combinatorial}\cite{cormen2001introduction}.

In order to prove that that VaR and the weighted median problem are equivalent, we first show that $\quant_{\alpha}^+(\tilde{x})$ is an element in the array. 
\begin{lemma}\label{lem:quantile-in-finite-omega}
Suppose that $\tilde{x}\colon \Omega \to \Real$ is a random variable with finite $\Omega$. Then, there exist $\omega_+, \omega_-\in \Omega$ such that $\quant_{\alpha}^+(\tilde{x}) = \tilde{x}(\omega_+)$ and $\quant_{\alpha}^-(\tilde{x}) = \tilde{x}(\omega_-)$ for each $\alpha\in (0,1)$.
\end{lemma}

We now define our version of the Weighted Median problem.
\begin{definition} \label{WM_Problem}
    
Let $n \in \mathbb{N}$, $\xi_1, \ldots, \xi_n \in \Real$, $w_1, \ldots, w_n \in \Real_{\geq 0}$, and $W \in \Real$ with 
\[
0 < W < \sum_{i=1}^n w_i.
\]
Then the $(w_1, \ldots, w_n \mid W)$-weighted median with respect to $(\xi_1, \ldots, \xi_n)$ is defined to be the unique number $\xi^*$ for which
\begin{equation}
\sum_{i : \xi_i < \xi^*} w_i \leq W < \sum_{i : \xi_i \leq \xi^*} w_i.
\label{WeightedMedianEquality}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Weighted Median Problem: } A natural number $n$, numbers $\ell_1, \ldots, \ell_n \in \Real$, $w_1, \ldots, w_n \in \Real^{\mathbb{C}}$,  
and a number $W$ with $0 \leq W < \sum_{i=1}^{n} w_i$.


\medskip

\textbf{Task:} Find the $(w_1, \ldots, w_n; W)$-weighted median with respect to $(\ell_1, \ldots, \ell_n)$.
\end{definition}
We note that our definition differs very slightly from the popular definition in \cite{korte2011combinatorial} in that the position of the $\leq$ and $>$ in \cref{WeightedMedianEquality} are swapped.

We now show that we can cast VaR as an instance of the Weighted Median problem
\begin{lemma}
The weighted median problem defined in \cref{WM_Problem} is equivalent to solving VaR when $W = \alpha$ for a discrete random variable $\tilde{x}\colon \Omega \to \Real$ with finite $\Omega$ such that the outcomes of the random variable $\xi_1, \ldots, \xi_n \in \Real$ and the weights $w_1, \ldots, w_n \in \Real^{\mathbb{C}}$ correspond to the probabilities of each outcome .
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
Let $\xi^*$ be the optimal solution to the weighted median problem and let $W = \alpha$. Then $\P{\tilde{x} < \xi^*} \leq \alpha < \P{\tilde{x} \leq \xi^*}$. Thus $\xi^*$ is optimal for \eqref{eq:var-definition} among all outputs of $\tilde{x}$. Since the optimal value for VaR must be an outcome of $\tilde{x}$ by \cref{lem:quantile-in-finite-omega}, $\xi^*$ then must equal $\quant_{\alpha}[\tilde{x}]$ and the proof concludes.

\end{proof}

Below we present an algorithm for computing VaR in linear time. The algorithm is a highly trivial modification of that in \cite{korte2011combinatorial} and homework problems such as in \cite{cormen2001introduction} to account for the slight alteration in definition.


\begin{lemma} \label{PROOFCORRECTNESS}
  \Cref{alg:quick-quantile} computes the optimal solution $\xi^*$ to the weighted median problem  when $W = \alpha_0$, $x^0$ represents the values and $p^0$ represent the weights.
  \end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
 Loop Invariant: Before and after every iteration $\xi^*$  will remain in the array $x^k$ at an index $i$ such that $ f_k \leq i \leq b_k $.
Before any iteration is executed: Every element in $x$ has an index $i$ such that $ f_0 \leq i \leq b_0 $, where $f_0=1$ and $b_0=n$. 

During the loop, there are 3 possible scenarios at each iteration:
\begin{enumerate}

\item[Case 1]: $\alpha_{k-1} < t_k$ 
 For all values $\xi^*$ at indices greater than $l_k, \sum_{i : \xi_i < \xi^*} w_i > W$ and thus can not be solutions to the weighted median problem. $f_k$ and $b_k$ are set accordingly to exclude these indices and thus the loop invariant holds.

\item[Case 2]:  $t_k + e_k \leq \alpha_{k - 1}$
For all values $\xi^*$ at indices less than or equal to $g_k, \sum_{i : \xi_i \leq \xi^*} w_i \leq W$ and thus can not be solutions to the weighted median problem. $f_k$ and $b_k$ are set accordingly to exclude these indices and thus the loop invariant holds.
  .

\item[Case 3]: $\alpha_{k - 1} \geq t_k$ and $\alpha_{k-1} < t_k + e_k $. 
We know for the value $\xi^*$ at indice $g_k$, $\sum_{i : \xi_i < \xi^*} w_i \leq W < \sum_{i : \xi_i \leq \xi^*} w_i$ holds true. Thus this is the solution to the weighted median problem, and accordingly $f_k$ and $b_k$ are both set to $g_k$. 


At termination, $b_k=f_k$ will point to a single index. All other indices can not be a solution to the weighted median problem, hence this will be the optimal solution. Thus the loop invariant holds and the proof concludes.
 
\end{enumerate}
\end{proof}

\section{Computing \texorpdfstring{$\varphi$}{phi}-divergence Risk Measures in Linear Time} \label{sec:phi-divergence}


In this section, we propose new linear-time algorithms for computing $\varphi$-divergence risk measures. We approach this challenge by solving a linear optimization problem over polymatroids . As we show, for certain polymatroids this optimization can be solved in linear time compared to the $O(n \log  n)$ runtime of standard sorting based greedy algorithms. 

First, recall the standard definition of submodular functions and the related concept of polymatroid~\cite{Korte2012}.
\begin{definition} \label{def:polymatroid}
A function $f \colon 2^{\mathcal{E}} \to \Real_{\ge 0}$ for a finite set $\mathcal{E} = \left\{ 1, \dots , E \right\}$ and $E\in \Nats$ is \emph{submodular} if
\[ 
  f(\mathcal{A}) + f(\mathcal{B}) \geq f(\mathcal{A} \cup \mathcal{\mathcal{B}}) + f(\mathcal{A} \cap \mathcal{B}),
  \quad
  \forall \mathcal{A}, \mathcal{B} \subseteq \mathcal{E}.
\]
A \emph{polymatroid} $\mathcal{Q}_f$ for $f$ is a polytope defined as
  \[
    \mathcal{Q}_f := \left\{ q \in \Real_{\ge 0}^E  \mid   q(\mathcal{U}) \leq f(\mathcal{U}), \, \forall \,  \mathcal{U} \subseteq \mathcal{E} \right\},
  \]
  where $q(\mathcal{U}) := \sum_{e \in \mathcal{U}} q(e)$. %and $f(\mathcal{U})\rightarrow[0, 1]$. We note that condition $f(\mathcal{U})\rightarrow[0, 1]$ is not necessary in general, but is needed in the context of probability distributions.
  \mm{Do we need to assume that $f(\emptyset) = 0$?}
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition} \label{prop:coherent-polymatroid}

\begin{equation}\label{eq:comontonic-optimization}
   \rho[\tilde{x}] = \min_{\bm{q}\in \mathcal{Q}_c} \bm{q}\tr  \bm{x},
\end{equation}

\end{proposition}

\end{definition}

\subsection{CVaR}
We now prove that CVaR can be represented as a polymatroid with a submodular function that satisfies Element-Wise-Seperability as defined above. Therefore we can provably compute CVaR in expected linear time.

\begin{proposition}
    

Let $f\colon 2^{\Omega} \to \Real_{\ge 0}$ be defined as
\[
  f(\mathcal{O})
  :=
  \min\left\{\frac{1}{\alpha} \cdot \P{\mathcal{O}}, 1\right\},
\quad \forall \mathcal{O} \subseteq \Omega .
\]
Then $f$ is submodular and the polymatroid $\mathcal{Q}_f$ satisfies
\[
\cvar{\alpha }{\tilde{x} } = \max \left\{\bm{x}\tr \bm{q} \mid  \bm{q} \in \mathcal{Q}_f\right\}.
\]
Moreover $f$ also satisfies Element-Wise-Separabaility with $m = 1/\alpha$ and $c = 0$ 
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}

% Note: From here on, we will use $\setminus$ to denote the complement of a set.

We first show that for any $q$ in $\mathcal{Q}_f$, $q(\mathcal{O})$ $\leq f(\mathcal{O})$. This directly follows from the constraint set in definition 2 of CVaR where we see that $q_i \leq ((1/\alpha)\cdot p_i)$ for any element $i$ in the distributions $p$ and $q$.
  

We also show below, we can construct $q$ such that $q(\mathcal{O}) =\min\left\{\frac{1}{\alpha} \cdot \P{\mathcal{O}}, 1\right\} $ for any $\mathcal{O}$. 
\item[Case 1]: $\P{\mathcal{O}} \geq \alpha$. 
In this scenario, we aim to construct $q(\mathcal{O})$ = 1 since $\frac{1}{\alpha}\cdot \P{\mathcal{O}} \geq 1 $. Thus, every $q_i$ such that $i$ represents an element in $\mathcal{O}$ should be set to $p_i \cdot \frac{1}{\P{\mathcal{O}}}$ and all other $q_i$ should be set to 0 . Since  $\P{\mathcal{O}} \geq \alpha$, we can imply the reciprocal $\frac{1}{\P{\mathcal{O}}} \leq \frac{1}{\alpha}$ is also true given $\P{\mathcal{O}} \geq \alpha > 0$. Therefore, every $q_i$ is feasible given definition 2.

\item[Case 2]: $\P{\mathcal{O}} < \alpha$. 
In this scenario, since $\frac{1}{\alpha} \cdot  \P{\mathcal{O}} < 1$ . Then we construct $q$ such that $q(\mathcal{O}) = \frac{1}{\alpha} \cdot  \P{\mathcal{O}}$. 
  Thus, every $q_i$ such that $i$ represents an element in $\mathcal{O}$ should be set to $\frac{1}{\alpha} \cdot p_i$ and $p_i \cdot  d$ for all other $i$ such that $\frac{1}{1 - \alpha} \cdot \P{\mathcal{O}} + d \cdot \P{\mathcal{O}\setminus} = 1$, where $ \mathcal{O}\setminus$ contains all elements not in $\mathcal{O}$. 
  
  We now show  that $d \leq \frac{1}{\alpha}$, which is required for the constructed $Q$ to be feasible. For all feasible $\alpha \geq 0$, $\frac{1}{\alpha} \geq 1$. Since $\P{\mathcal{O}} + \P{ \mathcal{O}\setminus} = 1$,  $d \leq 1$ in order for $\frac{1}{\alpha} \cdot  \P{\mathcal{O}} + d\cdot\P{\mathcal{O}\setminus} = 1$ to hold. Since $1 \leq \frac{1}{\alpha}$, this implies $d \le \frac{1}{\alpha}$.
  
  
  We have now shown that $q( \mathcal{O}) \leq f(\mathcal{O}) = \min\left\{\frac{1}{\alpha} \cdot \P{\mathcal{O}}, 1\right\}$ and it is possible to construct $Q(\mathcal{O})$ such that $Q(\mathcal{O}) = \min\left\{\frac{1}{\alpha} \cdot \P{\mathcal{O}}, 1\right\}$. Thus by Remark 4.90 in \cite{Follmer2016}, $f(\mathcal{O}) = \min\left\{\frac{1}{\alpha} \cdot \P{\mathcal{O}}, 1\right\}$
\end{proof}


\subsection{TVaR}
We remind the reader that in this work we define TVaR as the $\varphi$-divergence risk that uses the $L_1$ norm divergence. This may not seem very interpretable, however we can show that we can set the divergence constraint, or $\beta$, such that it can be a provable lower bound for a given EVaR with $\alpha$ value between 0 and 1.
From previous work , we know that the KL divergece is $\leq \frac{1}{2 \log(2)} \, \|p - q\|_1^2$ \cite{Iyengar2005}. Thus, by algebraic manipulation it can be seen that the coherent risk measure TVaR with the divergence constraint $\|p - q\|_1 \leq \sqrt{2 \log(2) *-\log(\alpha)}$  must be $\leq EVaR_\alpha[\tilde{x}]$. 

We now prove the submodular function for TVaR and show that it also satisfies ElementWiseSeperability as defined above.
\begin{proposition}
    

For $\mathcal{O} \subseteq \Omega$, define $f_{\beta}\colon 2^{\Omega} \to \Real_{\ge 0}$:
  \[
    f_{\beta}(\mathcal{O}) = \min\left\{\P{\mathcal{O}} + \frac{\beta}{2}, 1\right\}.
  \]
If $\mathcal{O} \neq \emptyset$, and $0$ otherwise, where $\beta = \sqrt{ 2 \log 2 \cdot \log \frac{1}{\alpha}}$ for $\alpha\in (0,1)$ represent the divergence constraint. Then
\[
  \tvar{\alpha }{\tilde{x} } = \max \left\{\bm{x}\tr \bm{q} \mid  \bm{q} \in \mathcal{Q}_{f_{\beta}}\right\}.
  \]
$f_{\beta}$ also satisfy ElementWiseSeparability with $m = 1$ and $c = \beta/2$.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
 
  We first note that $\mathcal{O} = \emptyset$, then $Q(\mathcal{O}) = 0$,   Due to the set of constraints, we know that for all $ \mathcal{O} \text{ such that } \mathcal{O} \neq \emptyset,  q$ must be such that $Q({\mathcal{O}}) \leq {\P{\mathcal{O}}} + \frac{\beta}{2}$ as if $Q(\mathcal{O}) \geq \frac{\beta}{2} +  \P{\mathcal{O}}$ , then $Q(\mathcal{O'})$ must be $< \P{\mathcal{O}} - \frac{\beta}{2}$ in order for the sum constraint of the simplex to equal 1, thus violating the l1 Norm constraint.
  
  Furthermore, as we show below, we can construct $q$ such that  for any $\mathcal{O} \neq \emptyset$,   
  \[f(\mathcal{O}) = \min\left\{\P{\mathcal{O}} + \frac{\beta}{2}, 1\right\}
    .
  \].
  
\item[Case 1]: $\P{\mathcal{O}} + \frac{\beta}{2} \geq 1$. In this scenario, we pick an element in $\mathcal{O}$ and add $1 - \P{\mathcal{O}}$ to it. We set all elements not in $\mathcal{O}$ to 0.

\item[Case 2]: $\P{\mathcal{O}} + \frac{\beta}{2}< 1$. In this scenario, we pick an element in $\mathcal{O}$ and add $\frac{\beta}{2}$ to it. We set all elements not in $\mathcal{O}$ to 0.

We have now shown that $Q(\Omega) \leq f(\mathcal{O}) = \min\left\{\frac{1}{\alpha} \cdot \P{\mathcal{O}}, 1\right\}$ and it is possible to construct $Q(\Omega)$ such that $Q(\Omega) = \min\left\{\frac{1}{\alpha} \cdot \P{\mathcal{O}}, 1\right\}$. Thus by Remark 4.90 in \cite{Follmer2016}, $f(\mathcal{O}) = \min\left\{\frac{1}{\alpha} \cdot \P{\mathcal{O}}, 1\right\}$
\end{proof}

\subsection{Proof of \cref{lem:quantile-in-finite-omega}}
\begin{proof}
Recall the definition:
 \begin{align} \label{eq:var-definition-close}
  \quant_{\alpha}^+(\tilde{x})
  \;:=\;
    \max\; \left\{\tau \in \Real \mid \P{ \tilde{x} < \tau } \le \alpha\right\},
 \end{align}

Let $x[\omega_1] \leq x[\omega_2] \leq \dots \leq x[\omega_n]$ without loss of generality. Suppose for the sake of contradiction that there is no $\omega\in \Omega $ such that $\quant^+_{\alpha}(\tilde{x}) = \tilde{x}[\omega]$. Then since $\quant^+_{\alpha}(\tilde{x}), x[\omega] \in \Real$ and $\Omega$ is finite, there are only 3 cases to consider.

\begin{enumerate}
\item[Case 1]: $\quant^+_{\alpha}(\tilde{x}) = \tau\opt \in (x[\omega_i], x[\omega_{i + 1}])$.
Since there are no $\omega\in \Omega$ such that $x[\omega] \in  [\tau\opt , x[\omega_{i+1}])$:

  \[
    \P{\tilde{x} < x[\omega_{i+1}]} = \P{\tilde{x} <\tau\opt} \le \alpha.
  \]
Therefore, $x[\omega_{i+1}] > \tau\opt$ is feasible in \eqref{eq:var-definition-close} contradicting the optimality of $\tau\opt$.
\item[Case 2]: $\quant^+_{\alpha}(\tilde{x}) = \tau\opt < x[\omega_1]$. Since there is no $\omega\in \Omega$ such that $x[\omega] \in (-\infty, x[\omega_1])$:
  \[
    \P{\tilde{x} < x[\omega_{1}]} = \P{\tilde{x} <\tau\opt} = 0 \le \alpha.
  \]
  Therefore, $x[\omega_1] > \tau\opt$ is feasible in \eqref{eq:var-definition-close} contradicting the optimality of $\tau\opt$.

\item[Case 3]: $\quant^+_{\alpha}(\tilde{x}) = \tau\opt > x[\omega_n]$.
  For all $\tau \ge \tau\opt$
  \[
    \P{\tilde{x} < \tau } \ge  \P{\tilde{x} < \tau\opt } = 1 > \alpha.
  \]
  Therefore, $\tau\opt$ cannot be optimal in~\eqref{eq:var-definition-close} since $\alpha < 1$. %, which contradicts the hypothesis.
  
\end{enumerate}
By similar reasopning one can show that $\quant_{\alpha}^-(\tilde{x})$ is also in the set of values of $\tilde{x}$, i.e. there exists $\omega\in \Omega$ such that $\quant_{\alpha}^-(\tilde{x}) = \tilde{x}[\omega]$.
\end{proof}


\subsection{Standard VaR Algorithm} \label{sec:stand-var-algor}

\begin{algorithm}
\caption{VaR: Standard VaR algorithm}
\label{alg:slow-quantile}
\Input{vector $\bm{x}$, probability $\bm{p}$, and $\alpha \in [0,1)$}
\Output{$\alpha$-quantile of $\tilde{X}$}
Sort $\bm{x}$ indexes to get $\bm{k}\in \Nats^n$ such that $i > j \implies x_{k_i} > x_{k_j}, \; \forall i, j \in 1, \dots , n$\;
$q_{n+1} \gets 0$ \;
\For{$i=n, \dots , 1$} {
    $q_i \gets q_{i+1} + p_{k_i}$
    \tcp*{$q_i = \P{\tilde{x} \ge x_{k_i}}$ if $x_{k_i}$ unique}
    \lIf{$q_i \ge 1-\alpha$} {\KwRet{$x_{k_i}$}}
}
\end{algorithm}

\begin{proposition}
For a random variable $\tilde{x} \in \mathbb{X}$ and $\alpha\in [0,1)$, \cref{alg:slow-quantile} returns $\var{\alpha}{\tilde{x}}$.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
First, we show by induction on $i$ from $n$ to $1$ that
\begin{equation} \label{eq:proof-q-j}
  q_j = \P{\tilde{x} \ge x_{k_i}},
  \quad\text{where}\quad
  j = \min \left\{ j = 1, \dots , i \mid x_{k_j} = x_{k_i} \right\}.
\end{equation}
The base case is immediate. The inductive case for $i-1$ from $i$ follows by algebraic manipulation from the fact that $x_{k_i}$ is sorted increasingly:
\begin{align*}
\P{\tilde{x} \ge x_{k_{i-1}}} 
 &= \P{\tilde{x} = x_{k_{i-1}}} + \P{\tilde{x} > x_{k_{i-1}}}
 = \P{\tilde{x} = x_{k_{i-1}}} + \P{\tilde{x} \ge x_{k_{i}}} \\
 &= \P{\tilde{x} = x_{k_{i-1}}} + q_i = \sum_{l=j}^{i-1} q_{k_j} + q_{k_i} = q_{k_{i-1}}.
\end{align*}
Now, suppose that \cref{alg:slow-quantile} terminates with value $q\opt$. Then, if the algorithm terminates in iteration $i\opt$ then from \eqref{eq:proof-q-j}, we have that $\P{\tilde{x} \ge q\opt} = q_{j\opt} \ge 1-\alpha$ where $j\opt$ is defined as in \eqref{eq:proof-q-j}. Therefore, $q\opt \le \var{\alpha}{\tilde{x}}$. To prove the converse inequality, suppose that $q\opt > \var{\alpha}{\tilde{x}}$, and therefore $\P{\tilde{x} \ge q\opt} < 1-\alpha$. Then, this is a violation since \eqref{eq:proof-q-j} combined with the termination criterion implies that $\P{\tilde{x} \ge q\opt} < 1-\alpha$.
\end{proof}

\begin{algorithm}
  \caption{\texttt{partition}: Dutch national flag algorithm ~\cite[chapter 14]{Dijkstra1976}}
  \label{alg:partition}
  \Input{Random variable $\bm{x} \in \Real^n$ with probability mass function $\bm{p} \in \Delta^n$, pivot value $v\in \Real$, and front and back indices $f, b \in \Nats$}
  \Output{indices $l$, $g$ and $\bm{x}$ and $\bm{p}$ such that
    $x_j < v, \, j\in f{:}(l-1), \quad x_j = v, \, j \in l{:}g, \quad x_j > v, \, j \in (g+1){:}b $}
  % $i \gets f + \lceil\frac{b-f}{2}\rceil$ \tcp*{Pivot: Median-of-medians for $O(b-f)$ runtime}
  $l_0 \gets f$ \tcp*{invariant: less: $x_j < v, \, j\in f{:}(l-1)$}
  $e_0 \gets f$ \tcp*{invariant: equal: $x_j = v, \, j \in l{:}(e-1)$ }
  $g_0 \gets b$ \tcp*{invariant: greater: $x_j > v, \, j \in (g+1){:}b$}
  $k \gets 0$ \;
  
  \While{$e_k \leq g_k$}
  {
  \label{line:partition-while}
    \tcp{invariants hold here}
    \If{$x_{e_k} < v$}{
      swap: $x_{e_k} \leftrightarrow x_{l_k}$ and $p_{e_k} \leftrightarrow p_{l_k}$\; \label{line:swap1}
      $l_{k+1} \gets l_k + 1$ \;
      $e_{k+1} \gets e_k + 1$\; \label{line:partition-e-1-1}
    }
    \ElseIf{$x_{e_k} > v$}{
      swap: $x_{e_k}\leftrightarrow x_{g_k}$ and $p_{e_k} \leftrightarrow p_{g_k}$\; \label{line:swap2}
      $g_{k+1} \gets g_{k} - 1$\; \label{line:parition-g-1}
    }
    \lElse {$e_{k+1} \gets e_{k} + 1$} \label{line:partition-e-1-2}
    $k \gets k + 1$
  }
  \KwRet{$(l_k, g_k, \bm{x}, \bm{p})$}\; 
  %\tcp{last element less than the pivot, first element greater than the pivot}
\end{algorithm}


\begin{lemma} \label{lem:hoare-partition}
Suppose that for $\bm{x}'\in \Real^n$, $\bm{p}'\in \Delta^n$ and $f, b\in \Nats$ such that $f < b$ $(l, g, \bm{x}, \bm{p}) \gets \operatorname{partition}(\bm{x'}, \bm{p'}, i, f, b)$ is computed using \cref{alg:partition}. Then:
\begin{align}
   j \in f{:}(l-1) &\quad\implies \quad x_j < x_i', \label{eq:hoare-less-than}\\
   j \in l{:}g &\quad\implies \quad x_j = x_i', \label{eq:hoare-equal-to}\\
   j \in (g+1){:}b &\quad\implies \quad x_j > x_i', \label{eq:hoare-greater-than}\\
   \forall \; j, j' \in [1\dots n]&\quad x_j = x'_{j'} \iff p_j = p'_{j'}. \label{eq:hoare-correspond}
\end{align}
Moreover, the algorithm runs in $O(b - f)$ time.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
Let $v$ be an input to \cref{alg:partition} and consider the following invariants.
\begin{align}
\label{eq:ho-inv-less}   x_j &< v, & j\in f_k{:}(l_k-1), \\
\label{eq:ho-inv-equal}  x_j &= v, & j \in l_k{:}(e_k-1), \\
\label{eq:ho-inv-more}   x_j &> v, & j \in (g_k+1){:}b_k.
\end{align}
  
\emph{Initialization}. For $k=0$, conditions \eqref{eq:ho-inv-less}, \eqref{eq:ho-inv-equal}, \eqref{eq:ho-inv-more} are true because the intervals are empty. 
  
\emph{Maintenance}. At each iteration of the loop that starts on line \ref{line:partition-while} of \cref{alg:partition} there are 3 possible branches determined by the conditional logic. $x_{e_k} < v$, $x_{e_k} > v$ and $x_{e_k} = v$. Since $v, x_{e_k} \in \Real$ one of these branches must be executed for each iterate $k$.

  Each time the $x_{e_k} < v$ or $x_{e_k} > v$ branches are executed conditions \eqref{eq:ho-inv-less} and \eqref{eq:ho-inv-more} remain true respectively since in each case we are swapping neccecary elements to preserve each condition. Notice also that $e_k$ is incremented alongside $l_k$ to maintain $e_k - l_k = e_{k+1} - l_{k+1}$ as is necessary for condition \eqref{eq:ho-inv-equal}.

  When $x_{e_k} = v$, $e_k$ is incremented, since $e_0 = l_0$ this creates a difference between $e_{k+1}$ and $l_{k+1}$, the algorithm terminates when $g_t = e_t$, $g_t - l_t = e_t - l_t > 0$ therefore \eqref{eq:ho-inv-equal} remains true.   

  Condition \eqref{eq:hoare-correspond} remains true for each mutation that may occur as both the $x$ values and $p$ values are swapped at the same indices, see lines \ref{line:swap1} and \ref{line:swap2}.
  
  \emph{Termination}. During the loop on line \ref{line:partition-while} there are 3 possible paths of execution determined by the conditional logic $x_{e_k} < v$, $x_{e_k} > v$ and $x_{e_k} = v$. Each of these paths of execution decreases $g_k - e_k$ by 1. This occurs on lines \ref{line:partition-e-1-1} and \ref{line:partition-e-1-2} by increasing $e_k$ by 1 and keeping $g_k$ the same. Or on line \ref{line:parition-g-1} by decreasing $g_k$ by 1 and keeping $e_k$ the same.
  
  $g_0 - e_0 = b - f$ is positive since $f<b$ by assumption. $g_k-e_k$ decreases by 1 at each iteration, and each iteration requires constant time to complete. The function returns when $g_t - e_t = 0$, therefore this algorithm completes in $O(b-f)$ time. 

  Notice on the last iteration $g_{t} = e_{t}$, therefore by the same reasoning as above $g_{t+1} - e_{t+1} = -1$ and 
  condition \eqref{eq:ho-inv-equal} holds for $g_{t+1} = e_{t+1} - 1$.
  
\end{proof}
\section{Proofs and Details for \cref{sec:phi-divergence}}
\begin{lemma}
This lemma is Theorem 4.5.1 from \cite{Bilmes18}: Given a ground set $V$. The following two are equivalent:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item For all modular functions $m \colon  2^V \rightarrow \Real_{\ge 0}$, the function $f\colon  2^V \rightarrow \Real$ defined as
    \[
    f(A) = g(m(A))
    \]
    is submodular.
    \item $g \colon  \Real_{\ge 0} \rightarrow \Real$ is concave.
\end{enumerate}

\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Note the proof is not the same provided in \cite{Bilmes18}, but we feel this proof is easier. It is well known that concave functions are subadditive - that is for any concave function $g$ and two values $y$ and $z$, $g(y) + g(z) \geq g(y + z)$ on the interval $[0, \infty)$. Given $A \subseteq B \subseteq E $, it follows $0 \leq a = m(A) \leq b = m(B)$. Therefore it follows that $f(A) + f(B) = g(a) + g(b) \geq g(a + b) = f(A \cup B)$. Thus it follows from the definition of sub-modularity that $f$ is submodular.

\end{proof}
