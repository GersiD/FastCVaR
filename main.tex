\begin{abstract}
Monetary risk measures have gained popularity in expressing decision-makers' preferences for risk aversion. Value-at-Risk (VaR) and Conditional-Value-at-Risk (CVaR) are very common risk measures. This paper proposes novel algorithms for computing these risk measures for discrete distributions in linear time. Our algorithms improve upon existing algorithms, which must sort the random variable by at least a logarithmic factor. We adapt the QuickSelect algorithm and combine it with a general polymatroid representation of $\varphi$-divergence risk measures. Numerical results show that our new algorithms offer an order of magnitude speed up for large domains, and a library implementation of the algorithms is available at \textcolor{green}{REDACTED}.     
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

\todo{citations}
Monetary measures of risk generalize the expectation operator to be able to represent the risk aversion of decision makers in domains that range from robotics~\cite{} to finance~\cite{} to infrastructure maintenance~\cite{}. Value-at-Risk~(VaR) and Conditional-Value-at-Risk~(CVaR) are the most popular risk measures and have been making inroads in reinforcement learning, machine learning, and decision-making~\cite{Petrik2024}. Machine learning and reinforcement learning algorithms typically must compute the risk measures for discrete random variables. Fast algorithms for computing risk measures are necessary in several machine-learning settings~\cite{}. For example, when used as part of an optimization algorithm, the risk measure may need to be evaluated in every iteration of the algorithm and can represent a significant computational bottleneck~\cite{}. 


%However, in many applications such as robotics, finance, and autonomous systems, solutions must account for performance with respect to worst case scenarios rather than simply maximizing the mean. Therefore, it has become a common practice to optimize policies with respect to a risk measure~\cite{Follmer2016}, which focuses on the tails of the reward distribution rather than the expectation. 

% what are we doing; our contributions
This paper proposes new fast algorithms for computing risk measures of discrete random variables. First, we propose a new linear-time VaR evaluation algorithm. Our VaR algorithm, which we call \emph{QuickQuantile}, builds on the well-known ideas of QuickSelect but generalizes it to quantiles. The worst-case linear-time upper bound of \emph{QuickQuantile} matches the linear-time lower bound~\cite{}. Second, building on \emph{QuickQuantile} we propose a general algorithm for evaluating some $\varphi$-divergence risk measures~\cite{}, which include CVaR and Total-Value-at-Risk (TVaR)~\cite{}. The algorithm, which we call \emph{QuickDivergence}, works by representing certain $\varphi$-divergence risk measures as a linear minimization over polymatroids. The linear worst-case runtime of \emph{QuickDivergence} also matches the known lower bounds on the runtime~\cite{}. 

There is limited prior work on fast algorithms for evaluating risk measures. The conventional VaR algorithm for a discrete random variable must sort its values before it can evaluate its risk. As a result, the sort's runtime, which requires   $\Omega(n \log n)$ operations where $n$ is the number of the probability mass function~(pmf) entries, dominates the runtime complexity of VaR. To the best of our knowledge at the time of writing, no prior work has proposed or studied linear-time algorithms for any of the above risk measures.

Coherent risk measures, like CVaR and TVaR, can be evaluated by solving convex optimization problems~\cite{Follmer2016, rockefeller1996variational}. Although most of these optimization problems are tractable, their time complexity is too large to be practical for applications where they are most needed. Current practical implementations rely on specific properties of these optimization problems to compute the solution efficiently. For example, all existing CVaR algorithms must sort the values of the random variable to compute its value~\cite{}, as in the case for VaR. The $\Omega(n \log  n)$ time complexity of the sort dominates the time complexity and prevents linear time computation. In this paper, we circumvent the complexity of sorting by developing no-sort algorithms for these risk measures and others.

Numerous efficient algorithms have been designed for structured optimization problems that are similar to CVaR and TVaR evaluations. Some example of such structured optimization problems are the projection onto the simplex~\cite{adam2019projectionscanonicalsimplexadditional, Wang2013Simplex}, projection on k-capped simplex~\cite{kcapped_ang}, linear optimization on the intersection of $L_p$ or $\varphi$-divergence balls and the probability simplex as arise in robust optimizations~\cite{}, optimization for $\varphi$-divergence risk measures~\cite{}, and on polytopes defined by submodular functions which are known as polymatroids~\cite{}. Many of these algorithms require sorting the input variables, which has $\mathcal{O}(n \log  n)$ complexity. There has been mention of the possibility of linear time algorithms however, we could find neither work describing nor studying them. One exception is \cite{Condat2016}, which proposes a linear-time algorithm for computing the projection onto the simplex, which does not leverage QuickSelect. 

Our algorithms' time complexity improves on the state of the art by a logarithmic factor. Although this improvement may seem unimportant,  it is an acceleration that is practically meaningful without any serious drawbacks. Our algorithms are simple and easy to implement with little added complexity compared with the state-of-the-art. Our numerical results show that they can lead to an order of magnitude speed up for realistic large problems, a significant speed-up for moderately sized problems, and match the performance of existing algorithms for small problems.

\section{Preliminaries}\label{sec:preliminaries}

In this work, we restrict our attention to random variables defined on finite probability spaces $(\Omega, 2^{\Omega}, \bm{p})$ where $\Omega := \left\{ 1, \dots , n \right\}$, $\bm{p} \in \Delta_n$, and $\Delta_n$ is the $n$-dimensional probability simplex. We use a tilde to indicate a random variable, such as $\tilde{x}\colon \Omega \to \Real$. The set of real-valued random variables is denoted by $\mathbb{X}$. Vectors are denoted with a lower-case bold font, such as $\bm{x} \in \Real^n$ and indexed as $\bm{x}_i \text{ for } i = 1, \dots , n$. We use $\tilde{x}$ and $\bm{x}$ interchangeably, such that $\bm{x}_\omega = \tilde{x}(\omega) \text{ for }  \omega \in \Omega$. Sets are denoted with calligraphic letters. Finally, we use $\bar{\Real} := \Real \cup \left\{ -\infty, \infty \right\}$ to denote the extended real line. 

\emph{Monetary risk measures} $\rho\colon \mathbb{X} \to \bar{\Real}$ generalize the expectation operator to account for different notions of uncertainty in the distribution of a random variable~\cite{Follmer2016}. In this paper, we assume that the risk measures are applied to random variables that represent rewards and that a higher risk value is preferable to a lower risk value. 

\emph{Value-at-Risk}~(VaR) is one of the most popular and interpretable risk measures~\cite{Follmer2016,Shapiro2014}. It is defined for $\alpha\in [0,1)$ for any $\tilde{x}\in \mathbb{X}$ as~\cite{Follmer2016}
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:var-definition}
  \varo_{\alpha}(\tilde{x})
  \;:=\;
    \max\; \left\{\tau \in \bar{\Real} \mid \P{\tilde{x} < \tau}  \le \alpha\right\} =
    \max\; \left\{\tau \in \bar{\Real} \mid \P{\tilde{x} \ge  \tau}  \ge 1 - \alpha\right\}
\end{equation}
The maximum in~\eqref{eq:var-definition} exists because $\tau \mapsto \mathbb{P}[\tilde{x} < \tau]$ is a lower semi-continuous function \todo{what about the second definition?}. It is easy to see that $\varo_0[\tilde{x}] = \ess \inf [\tilde{x}]$, and typically the risk measure is extended as $\varo_{1}[ \tilde{x} ] = \infty$, we adopt this extension. The \emph{quantile} $\quant_{\alpha}$ for $\tilde{x}$ is defined as the set
\[
  \quant_{\alpha}[\tilde{x}] := \left\{ z\in \Real \mid  \P{ \tilde{x} \le z } \ge \alpha , \P{ \tilde{x} \ge z } \ge 1-\alpha  \right\},
  \quad
  \quant^+_{\alpha}[\tilde{x}] = \max \quant_{\alpha},
  \quad
  \quant^-_{\alpha}[\tilde{x}] = \min \quant_{\alpha}.
\] \todo{we define the quantile here, but do we need it? our algorithm is mainly concerned with VaR.}

We also consider \emph{Conditional-Value-at-Risk}~(CVaR), a common risk measure, which is defined for a random variable $\tilde{x} \in \mathbb{X}$ and risk level $\alpha \in (0,1]$ as~\cite{Follmer2016}
\begin{equation} \label{eq:primal_cvar}
    \cvaro_\alpha(\tilde{x})
  := \max_{z \in \Real} \left(z - \alpha^{-1} \bm{p}\tr[z \cdot \bm{1} -\bm{x}]_+\right) 
  = \min \left\{\bm{q}\tr \bm{x} \mid \bm{q} \leq \alpha^{-1} \bm{p},\, \bm{q}\in \Delta_n \right\},
    %&= \min \left\{  \Ex{\bm{\xi}}{ \tilde{x}  } \mid
  %\bm{\xi} \leq \frac{1}{\alpha} p, \bm{\xi} \in \Delta^n \right\} \\
  %&= \max \left\{ z - \frac{1}{\alpha} \Ex{\bm{p}}{z-\bm{x}}_+ \mid z \in \Real\right\} \\
\end{equation}
where $\bm{1}$ is a vector of ones. CVaR is usually continuously extended as $\cvaro_0[\tilde{x}] = \ess\inf[\tilde{x}]$.

\emph{$\varphi$-divergence risk measures} generalize CVaR to a broader class of related risk measures~\cite{Ahmadi-Javid2017} as shown in \cref{tab:divergences}. A general divergence risk measure $\ER_{\varphi, \beta}\colon \mathbb{X} \to  \bar{\Real}$  is defined for risk parameters $\varphi\colon \Real \to \bar{\Real}$ and $\beta\in \Real_+$ as
\begin{align} \label{eq:fdiv-dual}
  \ER_{\varphi,\beta}(\tilde{x}) := \inf \left\{\bm{q}\tr \bm{x} \mid H_{\varphi}(\bm{q} \| \bm{p}) \le \beta, \bm{q} \ll \bm{p} \right\},
  \quad
  H_\varphi(\bm{q} \| \bm{p}) := \sum_{i \in \Omega} p_i \cdot \varphi\left(\frac{q_i}{p_i}\right),
\end{align}
where $H_{\varphi}$ is a $\varphi$-divergence~\cite{Bayraksan2015}, $\bm{q} \ll \bm{p}$ indicates that $\bm{q} \in \Delta_n$, and $p_i =0 \implies q_i = 0$ $\forall  i \in \Omega$. The function $\varphi$ is convex and satisfies $\varphi(1)=0$. Note that all $\varphi$-divergence risk measures also have the following equivalent dual representation~\cite{Ahmadi-Javid2011,Ahmadi-Javid2017}: 
\begin{equation} \label{eq:fdiv-primal}
\ER_{\varphi,\beta}(\tilde{x}) = \sup_{t,v \in \Real\,:\, t > 0}t \cdot \left(v - \E { \varphi\opt \left(- \frac{\tilde{x}}{t} + v + \beta \right)}\right) ,
\end{equation} 
where $\varphi\opt$ is the convex conjugate of $\varphi$. 

Finally, it is important to note that $\varphi$-divergence risk measures are coherent, which means they are convex and positively homogeneous. Any coherent risk measure $\rho\colon \mathbb{X}\to \bar{\Real}$ satisfies for some closed convex non-empty set $\mathcal{Q} \subseteq \Delta^n$ that~\citep[Prop. 2.84]{Follmer2016} 
\begin{equation*}
% \label{eq:correspondence_coherent_risk}
  \rho(\tilde{x}) =\inf_{\bm{q}\in \mathcal{Q}} \bm{q}\tr \bm{x}.
\end{equation*}

In the remainder of the paper, we first propose a linear-time algorithm for computing VaR then we build on it to propose a linear time algorithm for certain $\varphi$-divergence risk measures.

\section{Computing VaR in Linear Time} \label{sec:var-linear}

In this section, we develop a quick algorithm to compute the VaR of a discrete random variable. The algorithm extends the well-known QuickSelect algorithm to account to non-uniform weights. We also take a special care to allow random variables $\tilde{x}$ with duplicate values such that $\tilde{x}(\omega_1) = \tilde{x}$. This allows us to compute VaR in expected linear time as opposed to conventional algorithms such as A.1 in the appendix.



%We first restate some well-known properties of the Hoare Partition scheme.
Our \emph{QVaR} algorithm is described in \cref{alg:quick-quantile}. The algorithm works as follows. It first picks a pivot by taking the midde element between front and back and applies the partition scheme to easily compute the probability of all outcomes smaller than or equal to that of the pivot (Lines \ref{pivot_comp} - \ref{partion_line}). Note that three partitions are create, one where the elements are all less than the pivot, another containing all elements equal to the pivot, and a third pivot containing all elements greater than the pivot. We refer to these partitions as the left, middle, and right partition from here on. QVar then determines whether the quantile can exist in the left, middle, or right pivot (lines \ref{determine_left}, \ref{determine_right}, and \ref{determine_middle} ) and adjusts the $f_k$ and $b_k$ respectively to reduce the search space to one of these partitions. We note that in the case the quantile is determined to exist in the middle partition, the algorithm can terminate since every value in the middle partition is the same. In the case of $\alpha^0 = 0$, we handle this separately and simply find the minimum element.

 \[

\section{Computing \texorpdfstring{$\varphi$}{phi}-divergence Risk Measures in Linear Time} \label{sec:phi-divergence}


In this section, we propose new linear-time algorithms for computing $\varphi$-divergence risk measures. We approach this challenge by representing comonotonic coherent risk measures as linear optimization over a polymatroid. As we show, for certain polymatroids the optimization can be solved in linear time compared to the $O(n \log  n)$ runtime of standard sort-based greedy algorithms. 

First, recall the standard definition of submodular functions and the related concept of polymatroid~\cite{Korte2012}.
\begin{definition} \label{def:polymatroid}
A function $f \colon 2^{\mathcal{E}} \to \Real_+$ for a finite set finite set $\mathcal{E} = \left\{ 1, \dots , E \right\}$ is \emph{submodular} if
\[
  f(\mathcal{A}) + f(\mathcal{B}) \geq f(\mathcal{A} \cup \mathcal{B}) + f(\mathcal{A} \cap \mathcal{B}),
  \quad
  \forall \mathcal{A}, \mathcal{B} \subseteq \mathcal{E}.
\]
A \emph{polymatroid} $\mathcal{Q}_f$ for $f$ is a polytope defined as
  \[
    \mathcal{Q}_f := \left\{ \bm{q} \in \Real_+^E  \mid   \bm{q}(\mathcal{U}) \leq f(\mathcal{U}), \, \forall \,  \mathcal{U} \subseteq \mathcal{E} \right\},
  \]
  where $q(\mathcal{U}) = \sum_{e \in \mathcal{U}} q(e)$ and $f(\mathcal{U})\rightarrow[0, 1]$. We note that $f(\mathcal{U})\rightarrow[0, 1]$ is not generally required for polymatroids, but is necessary in the context of risk measures.
\end{definition}

We now describe an algorithm for optimizing over polymatroids with this class of submodular function.
\begin{algorithm}
  \caption{Linear-time Algorithm for \mm{optimizing linear function on polymatroids}}\label{alg:maximum-weight-independent}

  \Input{Finite $N = [1 \ldots n]$, $f\colon 2^N \to  \Real_+$ satisfying \cref{asm:submodular-function}, {vector $\bm{x}^0$, pmf $\bm{p}^0$}}
  \Output{$q_1, q_2, ... q_n$}
  
  \If{$c > 0$}
  {
    Swap $x_1, p_1$ with $x_j, p_j$ where $j = min_j(x_j)$
    
    $q_1 = f(p_1)$
    
    \If{$q_1 = 1$} {
      \KwRet{$q_1 = 1, q_i = 0$ for $1 < i \leq n$}
    }
   
  }
  $midval = \operatorname{QuickQuantile}(p, x, \alpha = (1 - c)/m) $
  
  Set $mid$ to smallest  $i$ such that $x_i = midval$
  
  \If{$c > 0$}
  {
    Let $q_i \gets  \begin{cases}m \cdot p_i & \text{for } 1 < i < mid \end{cases}$\;
    
  }
  \Else
  {
    Let $q_i \gets  \begin{cases}m \cdot p_i & \text{for } i < mid \end{cases}$\;
  
  }
  $sum = \sum_iq_i$
  
  $q_{mid} \gets 1 - sum$\;
  Let $q_i \gets 0 \text{ for } i > mid$
  
\end{algorithm}

We now prove the optimality of the algorithm and it's expected linear time complexity. The proof of optimality is obtained by showing that every value we compute for $q$ is equivalent to what would be obtained by following the computational procedure defined in \cite[Remark 4.86]{Follmer2016}, which is proven to be optimal. The proof of the time complexity follows from the expected linear time complexity, and the fact that remaining operations can trivally be computed in linear time, and thus have no impact on the complexity.


\begin{lemma}
  \cref{alg:maximum-weight-independent} returns a optimal solution to optimization problem defined in \cref{comontonic optimization}. 
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
The optimal solution will always be $q_{i+1} = f(\mathcal{O}_{i+1}) - f(\mathcal{O}_{i})$ \cite[Remark 4.86]{Follmer2016}, where $\mathcal{O}_i$ is the set of all elements that have values $\leq i$ .  For $f(\mathcal{O}_i+1) \leq 1$ and $f(\mathcal{O}_i) < 1$,\\


\begin{align*}
    f(\mathcal{O}_{i + 1}) - f(\mathcal{O}_i) &= c  +\sum_{i \subset (1, i + 1)}p_{i} - c  +\sum_{i \subset (1, i)}m *p(i)\\
    &= m *p(i + 1).
\end{align*}

For $i$ such that $f(\mathcal{O}_{i + 1}) = 1$ where $ (c  +\sum_{i \subset (1, i + 1)}m*p_{i}) > 1$ and $f(\mathcal{O}) \leq 1$, $f(\mathcal{O}_{i + 1}) - f(\mathcal{O}_{i}) = 1 - c  +\sum_{i \subset (1, i )}m *p_i$. We know that such $i$ is the largest $i$ that satisfies
$c  + m *\sum_{i \subset (1, i )}p_i \leq 1  $, which after algebraic manipulation results in the maximum $i$ such that $\sum_{i \subset (1, i )}p_i \leq (1 - c)/m $. We can thus see from \eqref{eq:var-definition} that VaR with $\alpha = (1 - c)/m$ will return the value at $x_{i + 1}$, which is calculated as $midval$ in the algorithm with $i + 1 = mid$. Thus, it follows that $q_{mid} = 1 - c  +\sum_{i \subset (1, i )}m *p_i$, where from the previous paragraph it follows that $ c  +\sum_{i \subset (1, i )}m *p_i = \sum_iq_i$. For all other $i > mid$, we note that $f(\mathcal{O}_{i}) - f(\mathcal{O}_{i - 1}) = 1 - 1 = 0$, and thus $q_i = 0$.
\end{proof}
\begin{lemma}
\cref{alg:maximum-weight-independent} run with expected complexity $\mathcal{O}(N)$.
\end{lemma}

\section{Experiments}\label{sec:experiments}

Our experimental design allows us to verify that our implementation performs well in practice rather than only theoretically. \cref{fig:all_uniform} shows the results of our experiments. The random variables were generated from a uniform distribution with $N$ samples, where $N$ is varied from $10^2$ to $10^6$. The algorithms were run for each value of $N$ 10 times, and the average runtime was recorded. For consistency, we used a risk level of $0.95$, however the results are similar for other risk levels save for $\alpha = 0 \text{ or } 1$.

The results in \cref{fig:all_uniform} represent a 95\% confidence interval band computed over 10 random runs, each with separately generated random variable. The confidence bands are tight due to the little variance in the measured runtimes between runs. Note that the figure shows the timing results in both linear and log scales.

The results confirm that our quick algorithms are significantly faster than their sorting based counterparts. Especially in the case of CVaR and VaR which are extensively used in the industries aforementioned in \cref{sec:intro}. As expected, the speedup is logarithmic in the runtime of the quick algorithms which is consistent with the theoretical complexity of the algorithms, see \cref{sec:appendix_experiments}. This log speed up coincides with a noticeable speed-up in runtime across the tested random variable sizes. 

The computation of risk measures is often done over large random variables where the runtime and sensitivity of the algorithms to the size of the random variable is a concern. The results presented in this section are chiefly concerned with demonstrating our ability to compute risk measures quickly, fulfilling the practical need.

\subsection{Standard VaR Algorithm} \label{sec:stand-var-algor}

\begin{algorithm}
\caption{VaR: Standard VaR algorithm}
\label{alg:slow-quantile}
\Input{vector $\bm{x}$, probability $\bm{p}$, and $\alpha \in [0,1)$}
\Output{$\alpha$-quantile of $\tilde{X}$}
Sort $\bm{x}$ indexes to get $\bm{k}\in \Nats^n$ such that $i > j \implies x_{k_i} > x_{k_j}, \; \forall i, j \in 1, \dots , n$\;
$q_{n+1} \gets 0$ \;
\For{$i=n, \dots , 1$} {
    $q_i \gets q_{i+1} + p_{k_i}$
    \tcp*{$q_i = \P{\tilde{x} \ge x_{k_i}}$ if $x_{k_i}$ unique}
    \lIf{$q_i \ge 1-\alpha$} {\KwRet{$x_{k_i}$}}
}
\end{algorithm}

\begin{proposition}
For a random variable $\tilde{x} \in \mathbb{X}$ and $\alpha\in [0,1)$, \cref{alg:slow-quantile} returns $\var{\alpha}{\tilde{x}}$.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
First, we show by induction on $i$ from $n$ to $1$ that
\begin{equation} \label{eq:proof-q-j}
  q_j = \P{\tilde{x} \ge x_{k_i}},
  \quad\text{where}\quad
  j = \min \left\{ j = 1, \dots , i \mid x_{k_j} = x_{k_i} \right\}.
\end{equation}
The base case is immediate. The inductive case for $i-1$ from $i$ follows by algebraic manipulation from the fact that $x_{k_i}$ is sorted increasingly:
\begin{align*}
\P{\tilde{x} \ge x_{k_{i-1}}} 
 &= \P{\tilde{x} = x_{k_{i-1}}} + \P{\tilde{x} > x_{k_{i-1}}}
 = \P{\tilde{x} = x_{k_{i-1}}} + \P{\tilde{x} \ge x_{k_{i}}} \\
 &= \P{\tilde{x} = x_{k_{i-1}}} + q_i = \sum_{l=j}^{i-1} q_{k_j} + q_{k_i} = q_{k_{i-1}}.
\end{align*}
Now, suppose that \cref{alg:slow-quantile} terminates with value $q\opt$. Then, if the algorithm terminates in iteration $i\opt$ then from \eqref{eq:proof-q-j}, we have that $\P{\tilde{x} \ge q\opt} = q_{j\opt} \ge 1-\alpha$ where $j\opt$ is defined as in \eqref{eq:proof-q-j}. Therefore, $q\opt \le \var{\alpha}{\tilde{x}}$. To prove the converse inequality, suppose that $q\opt > \var{\alpha}{\tilde{x}}$, and therefore $\P{\tilde{x} \ge q\opt} < 1-\alpha$. Then, this is a violation since \eqref{eq:proof-q-j} combined with the termination criterion implies that $\P{\tilde{x} \ge q\opt} < 1-\alpha$.
\end{proof}

\begin{algorithm}
  \caption{\texttt{partition}: Dutch national flag algorithm ~\cite[chapter 14]{Dijkstra1976}}
  \label{alg:partition}
  \Input{Random variable $\bm{x} \in \Real^n$ with probability mass function $\bm{p} \in \Delta^n$, pivot value $v\in \Real$, and front and back indices $f, b \in \Nats$}
  \Output{indices $l$, $g$ and $\bm{x}$ and $\bm{p}$ such that
    $x_j < v, \, j\in f{:}(l-1), \quad x_j = v, \, j \in l{:}g, \quad x_j > v, \, j \in (g+1){:}b $}
  % $i \gets f + \lceil\frac{b-f}{2}\rceil$ \tcp*{Pivot: Median-of-medians for $O(b-f)$ runtime}
  $l \gets f$ \tcp*{invariant: less: $x_j < v, \, j\in f{:}(l-1)$}
  $e \gets f$ \tcp*{invariant: equal: $x_j = v, \, j \in l{:}g$ }
  $g \gets b$ \tcp*{invariant: greater: $x_j > v, \, j \in (g+1){:}b$}
  \While{$e \leq g$}
  {
    \tcp{invariants hold here}
    \If{$x_e < v$}{
      swap: $x_e \leftrightarrow x_l$ and $p_e \leftrightarrow p_l$\;
      $l \gets l + 1$ \;
      $e \gets e + 1$\;
    }
    \ElseIf{$x_e > v$}{
      swap: $x_e\leftrightarrow x_g$ and $p_e \leftrightarrow p_g$\;
      $g \gets g - 1$\;
    }
    \lElse {$e \gets e + 1$}
  }
  \KwRet{$(l, g, \bm{x}, \bm{p})$}\;
  %\tcp{last element less than the pivot, first element greater than the pivot}
\end{algorithm}
